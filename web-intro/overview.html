This course aims to provide students from biology and the life sciences in general (e.g. biology, cell biology, biochemistry, immunology, physiology) with instruction in the basics techniques of bioinformatics, computational biology and data science.

Bioinformatics lies at the intersection of computer science and biology. The field is concerned with the development and use of software, databases and tools to make biological data (especially "-omics" data available). In this course, students will learn how to navigate bioinformatics tools including large centralized repositories such as the NCBI.

Computational biology also lies at the intersection of computer science and biology but this field is more concerned with the development of computational and statistical approaches to test biological hypotheses. Often this is through the use of machine learning techniques such as deep neural networks. In this course, students will learn the fundamental concepts such as supervised vs unsupervised learning, model fitting, generalization and over-learning, and cross-validation. They will build a simple artificially intelligent system in the course project.

Biological data science  lies at the intersection of biology, computer science and statistics. The field is concerned with developing and using methods to explore "big" data sets (large datasets are common in modern biology). In this course, students will learn data manipulation, descriptive statistics and visualization which will empower them to analyze complicated datasets.

The course assumes no previous experience in computer science, statistics or genomics, although a cursory knowledge certainly would assist here.

Students will leave this course with the ability to program in R, a computer language with a long history of application in Bioinformatics.

Students will learn specific techniques and tools in the analysis of DNA and amino acid sequence information (alignment, phylogeny), RNA expression (prediction of clinical end points in breast cancer), and biological pathway analysis. Within these applications, students will be introduced to some genomic technologies such as Next Generation Sequencing (NGS) with emphasis on DNA-and RNA-sequencing.

Additional concepts from computer science include: basics of programming, software versioning systems (eg GIT), cloud computing, recursion, and introductory aspects of algorithm design.
