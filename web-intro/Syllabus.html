Module 1 – The Basics.

Lectures 1: Information technology (IT) skills.

Goal: Better incorporate modern IT into our day-to-day operations as a life scientist.

Topics include:

  • Cloud sharing (Google Drive),
  • Cloud computing (Cloud Computing)
  • Project Planning (Trello),
  • Communication (Slack),
  • Code sharing (GitHub, BitBucket),
  • Integration between tools and sites (IFTTT),
  • Technical questions and advice (Stackoverflow),
  • Laboratory management (Quartyz, Benchling),
  • Literature management (Zotero),
  • Academic project management (LABSCRUM),
  • Notion for students and teachers (Notion for students and teachers),
  • Time management (Toggle for Time Management)

Lecture 2: Understanding modern computing paradigms

Goal: Components of modern computing with emphasis on concepts central to bioinformaticsm, computers versus information science. Biology as information.

Topics include: Cluster and cloud computing (AWS), Unix.

  • Stackoverflow
  • Antikythera Mechanism (youtube) (wiring diagram of gears)
  • Napier's bones (simulator)
  • Jacquard Loom
  • Babbage's machine (Video series episode 1 through 4)
  • History of Computers
  • History of Computer Science
  • from J Shallit (Waterloo, CS 134)
  • from J Kopplin
  • The Enigma, a biography of A Turing (The Imitation Game, movie)
  • MIT OpenCourseWare 

Lecture 3: Below the GUI: preparing to manage and develop software.

Goal: Installing R/RStudio. Operating systems such as Unix and Linux. GIT.

Topics include: command line operations, security, sharing, processes.

Lecture 4: Bioinformatic repositories with a focus on the NCBI.

Goal: To integrate large-scale centralized bioinformatic databasess into your toolkit as a life scientist.

Topics include:

  • The NCBI Handbook
  • PubMed (with a quick tour of European PubMed via an EBI video)
  • Google Scholar
  • National Centre for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) 
  • The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA)
  • International Cancer Genome Consortium (ICGC) and Pancancer Analysis of Whole Genomes ( (PCAWG) 

Lecture 5: Programming in R.

Goal: To learn programming in the context of the life sciences.

Topics include: installing the programming language R and associated interactive development environment (IDE) RStudio. variables, assignmnet, data types, operators, c, sets, vectors, matrices, arrays, lists

  • R Metabook
  • RStudio: An Interactive Development Environment for R
  • An Introduction to R
  • The R Programming Language
  • R Inferno
  • Advanced R (Good clean coverage of R)
  • Google's R Style Guide
  • The basic R manual (also available in Epub or html under “Manuals” from homepage)
  • A second on-line course for R basics from Datacamp.
  • Compilations of tutorials here and here
  • the R cheat sheet

Lecture 6: R factors, data.frames. Conditional execution. Looping.

  • R data import/export (under ‘Manuals’)
  • Bioconductor
  • A catalogue of BioC packages
  • Vignettes
  • Introduction to High Throughput Sequence Analysis in R/Bioconductor
  • Bioconductor YouTube Channel
  • Raphael Irizarry YouTube Channel
  • BioMart (R/BioC Package)

Lecture 7: How to organize your programs.

Goal: Learn how to create functions.

Topics include: arguments, parameters, modularization, scoping, black-box.

Lecture 8 (optional): Input/output in R.

Goal: Introduce students to basics of reading information in and out of R.

Topics include: csv files, data structures.

Module 2 - Exploratory Data Science

Lecture 1: Reproducibility in Science.

Goal: Goal: Explore some of the modern movements to make scientific studies, in particular life science including -omics and systems biology, reproducible.

Topics include: R markdown, Sweave (R), Jupyter notebooks (Python), NCBI repositories, open access journals, preprint servers.

  • Sweave and knitr with R
  • Jupyter
  • NCBI GEO
  • Public Library of Science (PLoS) Journals
  • bioRxiv

Lecture 2: Exploring gene expression in breast cancer

Goal: Develop skills from descriptive statistics to explore biological data.

Topics include: visualization, differential expression, clinical outcome, hypothesis testing (t-test).

  • JBStatistics video series on YouTube
  • Learn Statistics video series from Mike Marin (R specific)
  • Z scores (jbstatistics on YouTube)
  • t-distribution (jbstatistics on YouTube)
  • Using R with the t-distribution (jbstatistics on YouTube)
  • One-mean t-test (jbstatistics on YouTube)

Lectures 3: Unsupervised analysis: Discovery subtypes in breast cancer


Goal: Understand the concept of clustering and apply it to gene expression data.

Topics include: distance measures, hierarchical clustering, k-means clustering, visualization through heatmaps.

Lecture 4: Supervised Analysis: Linear Regression, a precursor to deep learning.


Goal: To build a classifier of patient prognosis using gene expression data from breast tumors.

Topics include: feature selection, linear models, classifiers, cost functions, optimization.

Module 3 – Computational Biology and Learning.

Lecture 1: Gene prediction and genome annotation.

Goal: Understand computational biology approaches to predicting genes in a genome, and learn a commonly used tool.

Topics include: Machine learning through Hidden Markov Models, probabilistic model, likelihood, accuracy.

  • GLIMMER
  • Hidden Markov Models (concise Nature review article)
  • HMMs the Movie
  • HMMs in R

Lecture 2: Phylogenetics.

Goal: Understand the basics of how species phylogenies are inferred computationally, and learn to use a popular tool.

Topics include: distance matrices, trees, neighbour joining, outgroups.

Lecture 3: Sequence Alignment.

Goal: Understand at a high level how pairs of sequences are alignment, and understand how to use common tools in the literature.

Topics include: scoring matrices, gaps, dynamic programming, BLAST, BLAST database search, DNA versus amino acid level alignments.

  • BLAST wiki.
  • The BLAST Handbook.
  • BioStars: BLAST.
  • BLAST using R (annotate package)
  • Multiple Sequence Alignment Tools
  • CLUSTAL
  • MUSCLE

Lecture 4: Proteins: Domains and Structure Prediction.

Goal: Understand the basics of structural and evolutionary approaches to defining subregions of proteins.

Topics include: substrings, secondary structure, evolutionarily conserved folds, HMMs.

Lecture 5: Deep learning.


Goal: An introduction to neural networks via extensions to logistic regression.

Topics include: artificial intelligence, neural networks, deep neural networks, autoencoders. FAST AI

Lecture 6: Deep learning: Image Analysis in Microscopy


Goal: To use introductory level convolution neural network to build a classifier for object recognition in microscopy.

Topics include: artificial intelligence, neural networks, deep neural networks, autoencoders. GPU Setup with Salamander
